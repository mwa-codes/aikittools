## Why Spreadsheets Stop Working After Week Two

Spreadsheets feel smart on day one. You name columns, color a few cells, and tell yourself this time will be different.

Then the grind hits. Manual entry is boring, so you skip a few rows “just for tonight.” Those nights stack up. Before long your sheet is half fiction.

There is no gentle nudge when a follow-up is overdue, no quick filter that says “show me who ghosted me last month,” and no built-in rhythm for status changes. You end up with a graveyard of roles that were already filled two weeks ago.

Worst of all, a spreadsheet does not tell you when to reach out again. You remember follow-ups when you are stressed or never. That is how qualified candidates quietly disappear — not because they failed, but because nobody stayed on top of the timing.

If you want a job application spreadsheet alternative that still feels lightweight, you need something built around status and dates — not endless cells you dread opening. One home base where you track job applications keeps follow-ups and titles from blurring together.

## What You Actually Need to Track During a Job Search

To track job applications without turning it into a second job, capture these six fields for every role:

**Company name and job title.** Employers blend together fast. Storing the exact title saves you when someone says “the coordinator role” and you have three coordinator postings open.

**Date applied.** Recruiters ask when you submitted. You should not guess. The date applied also anchors your follow-up timeline so you are not nudging people three hours after you hit send.

**Application status — Applied, Interview, Offer, Rejected, Ghosted.** Status turns your job search tracker into a dashboard instead of a list. You spot bottlenecks: lots of first screens but no seconds, or interviews that never convert.

**Contact or recruiter name.** When someone replies from a no-reply address with a real signature three threads deep, you want one place to tie the person to the pipeline stage.

**Follow-up date.** Pick a date you will check in if you have heard nothing — often about a week out, adjusted for how the posting reads. Without this field, follow-ups stay imaginary.

**Notes and next steps.** Jot what you submitted (portfolio link, version of your resume), questions you want to ask later, and reminders after calls. Those notes pay off when an interview gets scheduled and your brain is fried.

## How to Track Job Applications Step by Step

1. **Log every application the same day you apply.** Future-you is not more disciplined than today-you. Open your job application tracker right after you submit, while the posting is still on screen. Capture title, URL, and date in one pass. Yes, even if you applied from your phone at 11pm. Especially then.

2. **Set a follow-up date about seven days out immediately.** You are not spamming — you are reserving a slot to check in if the process stalls. Adjust if the listing says “no calls” or gives a hiring timeline.

3. **Update status after every interaction.** Got a phone screen? Move the row. Ghosted after a final round? Mark it. Your tracker only works if it reflects reality, not wishful thinking.

4. **Review your tracker every Monday morning.** Ten minutes, coffee in hand: scan follow-ups due this week, scan stale “Applied” rows older than ten days, and decide who gets a polite nudge. That weekly habit turns your tracker into your full picture.

5. **Use the notes field like a living cheat sheet.** Drop company-specific angles, names from LinkedIn, or “mentioned Salesforce cleanup project” after a call. When they invite you back, you sound prepared instead of vague.

You do not need perfect discipline — you need a repeatable loop. That loop is how you stay on top of thirty applications without it consuming your Sundays.

## Common Job Tracking Mistakes to Avoid

**Logging late.** If you only backfill your tracker on Sundays, you forget details and you postpone follow-ups. Same-day entry is the whole trick.

**Tracking company names without roles.** “Google” is not a row; “Google — Ads Ops Analyst” is. Otherwise you duplicate entries or confuse parallel applications.

**Skipping ghosted or stalled statuses.** Marking ghosted is not admitting defeat — it is data. Patterns tell you which channels waste time so you can rebalance effort.

**Never reviewing the sheet.** A job application tracker you never open is just a good intention with a URL. The Monday scan is what connects the tool to your week.
